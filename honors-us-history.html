Welcome to US History Honors

U.S. History Honors isn’t just about memorizing dates or names—it’s about discovering the story of how America became what it is today, and what that journey means for you. In this class, you’ll explore the triumphs, contradictions, struggles, and transformations that define the American experience. You’ll see that history isn’t frozen in textbooks—it’s alive, debated, and still unfolding.

At its heart, U.S. History is about people—dreamers, rebels, leaders, workers, students—just like you. It’s about the hard choices they faced, the risks they took, and the values they fought for. From the birth of the Constitution to the Civil Rights Movement, from westward expansion to World Wars, each unit of history raises big questions: What does freedom mean? Who decides what’s fair? How do we make progress?

This class gives you the tools to think critically and ask those big questions. You’ll analyze primary sources— actual letters, speeches, newspapers—and form your own interpretations. You’ll compare different viewpoints, examine cause and effect, and uncover how the past echoes in today’s headlines. In a world full of opinions, studying history trains you to seek truth through evidence.

U.S. History also helps you see how you fit into the bigger picture. It shows that change doesn’t just happen— it’s made, often by young people with courage and conviction. The story of America is still being written, and understanding the past empowers you to shape what comes next. Whether you’re passionate about justice, science, the arts, or leadership, history gives context to your purpose.

Yes, some parts of history are complicated, and others are painful—but facing those stories makes you wiser and more empathetic. It gives you a deeper appreciation for the freedoms you have, and a clearer vision of how to protect and improve them.

In U.S. History Honors, you’re not just studying a subject—you’re stepping into a conversation about who we are, where we’ve been, and where we’re going. This class challenges you to think like a historian and speak like a citizen. And along the way, you’ll discover that the past is full of people who weren’t so different from you— students with big dreams and bold questions.

Why Study US History?

To understand where we’re going, we must understand where we’ve been. US History is the story of revolutions, movements, resilience, and change. From the birth of a nation to the rise of modern America, you’ll explore how the ideals, struggles, and decisions of the past have shaped today’s society.

US History Honors is more than just names and dates — it’s a deep dive into the causes, consequences, and connections that define American identity and democracy.

What Is US History Honors All About?

This course offers a chronological journey through key events, ideas, conflicts, and people in American history. You'll analyze primary sources, engage in debates, and learn to think like a historian — asking not just "what happened?" but "why did it happen, and why does it matter?"

Major topics include:

  • Colonial America and the American Revolution
  • The Constitution and the early republic
  • Slavery, the Civil War, and Reconstruction
  • Industrialization, immigration, and reform
  • The World Wars and the Great Depression
  • Civil Rights and social movements
  • Modern America and global influence

Why Take US History Honors?

  • It Sharpens Your Thinking: Learn to evaluate sources, craft arguments, and detect bias.
  • It Prepares You for AP History Courses: Get a strong foundation for AP US History or Government.
  • It Connects to Current Events: Understand the historical roots of modern issues and debates.
  • It’s a Challenge Worth Taking: The Honors pace pushes you to think deeper and write better.

What to Expect

You’ll engage with:

  • Primary sources: Letters, speeches, laws, and historical documents
  • Essays and document-based questions (DBQs): Learn to write with evidence and structure
  • Discussions and debates: Explore multiple perspectives on controversial topics
  • Projects: Create timelines, presentations, or mock trials

How to Succeed in US History Honors

  • Read actively: Take notes, highlight causes and effects, and ask questions.
  • Stay organized: Keep track of events chronologically and thematically.
  • Think critically: Go beyond memorization — analyze, evaluate, and connect.
  • Practice writing: Develop clear thesis statements and support them with evidence.
  • Engage with the material: History is a conversation — be a part of it!

Periods of US History (with Films)

Pre-Colonial & Early Contact

Before European exploration, North America was home to diverse indigenous civilizations with complex societies, trade networks, and spiritual systems. Groups like the Iroquois, Pueblo, Mississippian, and others thrived with unique cultural identities. European contact, beginning with explorers like Columbus and continuing with Spanish, French, and English expeditions, had profound effects — spreading diseases, initiating trade, and beginning cycles of displacement and violence. This period introduces students to the foundational stories of both Native Americans and the European empires that would contest for control of the continent.

  • 500 Nations (1995, TV-PG)
  • America Before Columbus (2009, TV-PG)
  • Apocalypto (2006, R)
  • Black Robe (1991, R)
  • The New World (2005, PG-13)

Colonial America & Revolution

From the early 1600s through the 1770s, English settlers established colonies along the eastern seaboard, developing economies and local governments. Tensions with Britain grew due to taxation, trade restrictions, and lack of representation, leading to protest and, eventually, rebellion. The American Revolution (1775–1783) resulted in independence and launched the U.S. into nationhood. Key figures like George Washington, Thomas Jefferson, and Alexander Hamilton helped shape the early republic’s values and political institutions.

  • Hamilton (2020, PG-13)
  • John Adams (2008, TV-14)
  • The Patriot (2000, R)
  • The War That Made America (2006, TV-PG)
  • The Last of the Mohicans (1992, R)

Westward Expansion & Manifest Destiny

The 19th century was marked by U.S. expansion westward, driven by the belief in Manifest Destiny — the idea that Americans were destined to spread across the continent. Events like the Louisiana Purchase, Mexican-American War, and Indian Removal Act facilitated territorial growth but devastated Native communities. Railroads, homesteading, and the Gold Rush reshaped the landscape. This era includes triumphs of settlement but also the darker legacies of displacement and cultural erasure.

  • Into the West (2005, TV-14)
  • Dances with Wolves (1990, PG-13)
  • The U.S.–Mexican War (PBS) (1998, TV-PG)
  • Manifest Destiny (1994, TV-PG)
  • True Grit (2010, PG-13)
  • The Alamo (2004, PG-13)
  • Far and Away (1992, PG-13)

Slavery to Reconstruction

Slavery was central to the economy and society of the American South. The abolitionist movement gained strength in the 1800s, culminating in the Civil War (1861–1865). Following Union victory, the Reconstruction era (1865–1877) attempted to reunite the country and secure civil rights for freedmen. Constitutional amendments abolished slavery and granted citizenship and voting rights, but systemic racism and violent backlash undermined long-term progress, setting the stage for Jim Crow laws.

  • Roots (1977, TV-14)
  • 12 Years a Slave (2013, R)
  • Harriet (2019, PG-13)
  • Amistad (1997, R)
  • The Autobiography of Miss Jane Pittman (1974, TV-PG)

Civil War

The Civil War was the bloodiest conflict in U.S. history, fought between the Union and Confederacy over slavery and states’ rights. Major battles like Gettysburg and Antietam, and leaders like Lincoln, Grant, and Lee, shaped the outcome. The war preserved the Union and ended slavery but left deep national scars. Understanding this war is crucial to grasping the origins of racial and regional divisions that persist today.

  • Lincoln (2012, PG-13)
  • Gettysburg (1993, PG)
  • Glory (1989, R)
  • Ken Burns: The Civil War (1990, TV-PG)

Reconstruction

After the Civil War, the U.S. faced the daunting task of reunifying and redefining itself. Reconstruction policies aimed to rebuild the South and integrate formerly enslaved people into society. The 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments were transformative, but Reconstruction was short-lived. Federal troops eventually withdrew, and white supremacist groups reasserted control. This period set up the struggles of the Jim Crow era and the long fight for civil rights.

  • Slavery by Another Name (2012, TV-PG)
  • Freedom: A History of US (2003, TV-PG)
  • PBS: Reconstruction: America After the Civil War (2019, TV-PG)

Gilded Age

Spanning the late 1800s, the Gilded Age saw explosive industrial growth, urbanization, and immigration. Wealthy tycoons like Carnegie and Rockefeller dominated industries while workers faced poor conditions, sparking the rise of labor movements. Corruption plagued politics, and vast inequality characterized the era. Despite its name, the period’s “gilded” surface masked serious social issues that would lead to reforms in the Progressive Era.

  • The Men Who Built America (2012, TV-PG)
  • Newsies (1992, PG)
  • Matewan (1987, PG-13)
  • Iron Jawed Angels (2004, PG-13)
  • An American Experience: The Rockefellers (1999, TV-PG)

World War I & 1920s

The U.S. entered World War I in 1917, helping the Allies win. The war reshaped the global order and led to isolationist sentiment at home. The 1920s were a time of cultural transformation: jazz flourished, women gained the right to vote, and consumerism rose. But nativism, Prohibition, and racial tensions also defined the decade, making it both exciting and volatile.

  • The Lost Battalion (2001, TV-14)
  • The Great Gatsby (2013, PG-13)
  • Ragtime (1981, PG)
  • American Experience: The Great War (2017, TV-PG)

Great Depression

After the stock market crash of 1929, the U.S. entered a deep economic crisis. Unemployment soared, banks failed, and families lost homes and livelihoods. President Franklin D. Roosevelt’s New Deal introduced social programs and regulations to stabilize the economy and support citizens. The Depression reshaped the role of government and is a critical lens for understanding economic policy and reform movements.

  • The Grapes of Wrath (1940, G)
  • Cinderella Man (2005, PG-13)
  • Seabiscuit (2003, PG-13)

World War II

America’s involvement in World War II (1941–1945) began after the attack on Pearl Harbor. The U.S. fought on two fronts — Europe and the Pacific — and played a decisive role in defeating the Axis Powers. The war mobilized the entire population, including women and minorities, and spurred technological and industrial growth. The Holocaust, atomic bomb, and the birth of the United Nations all marked this turning point in global history.

  • Saving Private Ryan (1998, R)
  • Schindler’s List (1993, R)
  • Band of Brothers (2001, TV-MA)
  • A League of Their Own (1992, PG)
  • The War (2007, TV-PG)
  • The Imitation Game (2014, PG-13)
  • The Book Thief (2013, PG-13)
  • Flags of Our Fathers (2006, R)
  • The Boy in the Striped Pajamas (2008, PG-13)
  • Pearl Harbor (2001, PG-13)

Cold War

From 1945 to 1991, the U.S. and the Soviet Union engaged in a tense ideological rivalry. The Cold War shaped foreign and domestic policies through events like the Korean and Vietnam Wars, the Cuban Missile Crisis, and the Space Race. Fear of communism influenced politics, education, and culture. Though there was little direct combat between the superpowers, proxy wars and nuclear threats made this one of the most dangerous periods in modern history.

  • Thirteen Days (2000, PG-13)
  • Good Night, and Good Luck (2005, PG)
  • Bridge of Spies (2015, PG-13)
  • Hidden Figures (2016, PG)

Civil Rights & Social Movements

The mid-20th century saw a surge in activism to expand rights for African Americans, women, LGBTQ+ individuals, and workers. Landmark victories like Brown v. Board of Education and the Civil Rights Act of 1964 helped dismantle segregation. Leaders like Martin Luther King Jr., Malcolm X, and others led grassroots movements that challenged deeply rooted injustice. These decades reshaped American identity and law.

  • Selma (2014, PG-13)
  • Malcolm X (1992, PG-13)
  • Mississippi Burning (1988, R)
  • 13th (2016, TV-MA)
  • Walk the Line (2005, PG-13)
  • The Times of Harvey Milk (1984, PG)

1970s & Vietnam Era

The Vietnam War divided the nation, prompting massive protests and reshaping Americans’ trust in government. Simultaneously, the 1970s brought political upheaval with the Watergate scandal, economic troubles like stagflation, and cultural shifts in music, art, and attitudes toward authority. This was a time of reckoning and transformation that redefined what it meant to be American.

  • The Trial of the Chicago 7 (2020, R)
  • Born on the Fourth of July (1989, R)
  • All the President’s Men (1976, PG)
  • Frost/Nixon (2008, PG-13)
  • The Fog of War (2003, PG-13)

100 ChatGPT Prompts for AP U.S. History (APUSH)

1. Pre-Colonial & Early Contact (Before 1607)

  • What were some key cultural and regional differences among Native American societies before 1492?
  • How did geography influence the development of Native American civilizations?
  • What motivated European exploration in the 15th and 16th centuries?
  • What were the major effects of the Columbian Exchange on Native American populations and European society?
  • How did early contact between Europeans and Native peoples affect both groups?
  • How did Spanish colonization impact indigenous communities in the Americas?
  • What role did religion play in early European exploration and colonization?
  • How did Native Americans resist European encroachment and colonization?

2. Colonial America & Revolution (1607–1783)

  • How did life differ in the New England, Middle, and Southern colonies?
  • What role did religion play in shaping colonial values and communities?
  • What were the causes and effects of the French and Indian War?
  • How did the war change the relationship between Britain and the colonies?
  • What was the purpose of the Proclamation of 1763, and how did colonists respond?
  • Why did Britain begin taxing the colonies, and what were colonial responses?
  • What were the major causes of the American Revolution?
  • How did colonists organize resistance before declaring independence?
  • What were the key ideas in the Declaration of Independence?
  • What were the major challenges the colonies faced during the Revolutionary War?
  • What factors contributed to the American victory in the war?
  • How did the Revolution affect American society and government?

3. Westward Expansion & Manifest Destiny (1800–1850s)

  • What did Manifest Destiny mean, and how did it influence U.S. expansion?
  • How did westward expansion affect Native Americans?
  • What role did the federal government play in encouraging settlement of the West?
  • What were the causes and effects of the Mexican-American War?
  • How did new territories acquired during expansion fuel debate over slavery?
  • What challenges did settlers face as they moved west?
  • How did the Gold Rush impact the West and the nation’s economy?
  • How did railroads and the Homestead Act transform the American frontier?
  • How did western migration affect women, immigrants, and minority groups?

4. Slavery to Reconstruction (1800s–1877)

  • How did slavery shape life and the economy in the South?
  • What were some ways enslaved people resisted slavery?
  • How did abolitionist movements challenge slavery in the U.S.?
  • What events deepened the division between North and South?
  • What were the causes of the Civil War?
  • What were the main goals of Reconstruction after the Civil War?
  • How did life change for African Americans during Reconstruction?
  • What were the long-term effects of slavery and Reconstruction on the U.S.?

5. Civil War (1861–1865)

  • What were the main causes of the Civil War?
  • What advantages did the Union and Confederacy each have going into the war?
  • How did the Emancipation Proclamation affect the war’s purpose?
  • What was life like for soldiers and civilians during the war?
  • What were major turning points in the war?
  • How did the Civil War affect African Americans and enslaved people?
  • What were the consequences of the war for the federal government and the South?

6. Reconstruction (1865–1877)

  • What were the goals of Reconstruction, and how were they implemented?
  • How did the 13th, 14th, and 15th Amendments aim to protect African Americans?
  • What challenges did freedpeople face after the Civil War?
  • What was the role of the Freedmen’s Bureau during Reconstruction?
  • How did Southern resistance (like Black Codes and the KKK) affect Reconstruction?
  • Why did Reconstruction end, and what was its legacy?

7. Gilded Age (1877–1900)

  • How did industrialization change the American economy and workforce?
  • What problems did workers face, and how did they organize for change?
  • How did immigration and urbanization reshape cities?
  • What was the role of big business during the Gilded Age?
  • How did political corruption affect government at all levels?
  • What challenges did farmers face, and how did they respond?
  • What movements or reforms began in response to Gilded Age problems?

8. World War I & the 1920s (1914–1929)

  • What were the causes of World War I, and why did the U.S. eventually enter?
  • How did World War I affect life on the American home front?
  • What changes occurred for women and minorities during and after the war?
  • How did the U.S. economy and culture change during the 1920s?
  • What conflicts emerged between traditional values and modern ideas?
  • What was the significance of the Harlem Renaissance?
  • How did Prohibition impact American society?

9. Great Depression (1929–1939)

  • What were the main causes of the Great Depression?
  • How did the Depression affect families and communities across the U.S.?
  • What was the New Deal, and how did it seek to fix the economy?
  • How did the role of the federal government change under FDR?
  • What were some criticisms of the New Deal?
  • Did the New Deal end the Great Depression? Why or why not?

10. World War II (1939–1945)

  • What caused the U.S. to enter World War II?
  • How did the war affect American life on the home front?
  • What roles did women and minorities play during the war?
  • What were key battles and turning points in the war?
  • Why was the decision made to drop the atomic bomb, and what was the result?
  • How did World War II affect the U.S. economy and global position?
  • What were the long-term effects of the war on American society?

11. Cold War (1945–1991)

  • What were the causes of the Cold War between the U.S. and Soviet Union?
  • What was the policy of containment, and how was it used?
  • How did the Cold War shape U.S. foreign policy in places like Korea and Vietnam?
  • What was life like for Americans during the Cold War (e.g., Red Scare, civil defense)?
  • How did nuclear weapons and space exploration affect Cold War competition?
  • How did the Cold War eventually come to an end?

12. Civil Rights & Social Movements (1950s–1970s)

  • What were the main goals of the Civil Rights Movement?
  • How did different leaders (e.g., MLK, Malcolm X) shape the movement?
  • What were major legal and legislative victories for civil rights?
  • How did students and grassroots activists contribute to change?
  • How did other groups (e.g., women, Latinos, LGBTQ+, Native Americans) organize for rights?
  • What challenges remained after the Civil Rights Movement?

13. 1970s & Vietnam Era (1960s–1980s)

  • What caused the Vietnam War, and how did it affect the U.S. at home and abroad?
  • How did Americans respond to the Vietnam War and government policies?
  • What was the impact of the Watergate scandal on public trust in government?
  • What economic problems did the U.S. face in the 1970s?
  • How did American foreign policy change during this time?
  • What is the legacy of the 1960s–70s in terms of protest, politics, and reform?
